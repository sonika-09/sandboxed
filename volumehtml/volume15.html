When Machines Need ID Cards

There was a time when securing an organization was relatively straightforward. Most applications, servers, and employees operated within a single network, much like everyone working inside one office building. A firewall protected the entrance, and once someone stepped inside, they were generally trusted. That approach worked well when systems were centralized and interactions were limited.

Today’s technology landscape looks very different. Applications are spread across cloud platforms and powered by microservices, containers, APIs, and serverless functions. Instead of a single office building, organizations now resemble an interconnected city where thousands of digital interactions occur every second. Most of these conversations are not between people, but between machines working quietly behind the scenes to deliver the services we use every day.

For those conversations to remain secure, machines need identities of their own. These are known as Non-Human Identities (NHIs). Unlike employees who authenticate with passwords or multi-factor authentication, software workloads verify their identity with certificates, tokens, or cloud-managed credentials. Whether it is a Kubernetes pod requesting data or a CI/CD pipeline deploying new code, every workload must first answer a simple question: “Who are you?”

However, identity alone is not enough. Once a service proves who it is, it must also demonstrate that it has permission to perform the requested action. This is where service-to-service authorization becomes essential. An Order Service may be allowed to create a payment request but not issue refunds. An Inventory Service may access product information but should never be able to read payroll records. By granting only the permissions that each service genuinely requires, organizations reduce the risk of attackers exploiting compromised credentials.

This philosophy lies at the heart of Zero Trust. Every request is authenticated, authorized, and continuously evaluated, regardless of where it originates.

As organizations continue adopting cloud-native technologies and AI-driven systems, the number of Non-Human Identities will only continue to grow. Yet securing them remains one of cybersecurity’s newest frontiers. While standards, identity frameworks, and authorization models are advancing, there is still no universal blueprint for managing machine identities at scale. The challenge is no longer recognizing that NHIs need protection. It is determining how to secure millions of them without slowing the systems they enable. As the digital landscape continues to evolve, the answers are still being written, and the future of NHI authorization remains an open chapter for the cybersecurity community.
