Hacked at the Source: The Risk We Never Saw

An infamous ransomware group called Clop discovered hidden vulnerabilities in a popular tool, MOVEit, used by companies to securely send sensitive data. After stealing large volumes of information, the group blackmailed user companies, demanding ransom payments while holding the stolen data hostage. The implications were global, affecting government bodies, universities, banks, and major firms, given how widely MOVEit was relied upon.

The severity of the MOVEit Data Breach raises questions about how well user data is truly protected—and whether any organization should be trusted with it at all. In pursuit of cost-cutting and efficiency, firms often underinvest in protection systems, “getting by” while the real harm falls on users whose data is exposed. Some argue that firms already spend heavily on cybersecurity and that such attacks are inevitable. While this is partly true, incentives remain weak, and the damage from these attacks can certainly be reduced. When a single firm fails, the cost is borne by the entire ecosystem, often resulting in lasting customer mistrust.

It is therefore imperative for governments to enforce stricter laws and regulations around data protection. While such measures may create barriers to entry, the alternative cannot be inaction.
